# Suzuka Track Intelligence
Suzuka is a figure-eight layout and one of the most technically demanding circuits in F1. The high-speed S-curves in sector 1 impose sustained lateral loads on all four tires simultaneously, making it the most balanced four-tire-wear circuit on the calendar. Front stability through the S-curves and rear commitment at 130R define lap time.

Key sectors:
- Sector 1: First and Second Curve, S-curves (Esses). The S-curves are the most demanding sequence on the calendar — 8 direction changes in 20 seconds with minimal speed reduction. All four tires load heavily. Grip below 0.70 makes the S-curves progressively dangerous; the cliff is sharper here than anywhere else.
- Sector 2: Degner curves 1 and 2, Hairpin, Spoon curve. The hairpin is the key pit-strategy pivot — a lock-up under braking here flat-spots and creates vibration through the high-speed final sector. Spoon is a long-radius corner that generates high rear wear; monitor rear-left specifically.
- Sector 3: 130R, Casio Triangle (chicane), Start-Finish straight. 130R is the most committed flat corner in modern F1. If rear grip falls below 0.65, 130R becomes genuinely dangerous — instruct driver to lift marginally. A snap at 130R is race-ending.

Intervention notes: Suzuka wear is symmetric — front and rear wear at comparable rates, unlike most circuits. Balance is the priority. If axle imbalance exceeds 15% (front wearing significantly faster than rear or vice versa), adjust brake bias and throttle application style before considering early pit. Grip threshold for 130R: 0.65 — below this, instruct lift.
