RED BULL RING — Race Intelligence Guide
Circuit: Red Bull Ring, Spielberg, Styria, Austria
Type: Permanent | Laps: 71 | Avg Speed: ~215 kph | Lap Length: 4.326 km

CIRCUIT CHARACTERISTICS
Short circuit (4.326 km) set in the Styrian hills — the short lap means 71 laps and many more overtaking opportunities per race than other venues. Altitude (680m) affects engine power and brake cooling. Very traction-limited layout with multiple uphill acceleration zones. Medium downforce set-up. High-speed T3 (Remus corner) and T6 (Rindt corner) define the aero requirements. Austrian GP sometimes paired with Sprint weekend format.

TYRE BEHAVIOUR
Deg level: MEDIUM. Short lap means lower per-lap deg but accumulated over 71 laps the total is significant. Rear-dominated wear from traction zones at T2, T6, and T9. Front wear from T1 and T3 high-speed braking. The short lap and high number of laps means thermal deg accumulates steadily — a very slow puncture or early thermal issue has more laps to manifest. Medium and Hard compounds typical. Soft viable in sprint format (shorter stint). 2-stop race standard for 71 laps. Undercut window: laps 22-28 (first stop) and 45-52 (second stop).

SAFETY CAR HISTORY
Probability: HIGH (~65%). Short lap, close barriers at T1 and T4, and a narrow track relative to the speed create high incident probability. T1 braking zone (heavy downhill braking) sees lap 1 contact regularly. T4 hairpin contacts common throughout the race. Multiple SC periods in one race are frequent. Short lap means SC deployment window gaps are common — teams can be unlucky with pit timing multiple times in one race.

FAILURE MODES
Cooling: Cars running in traffic at altitude face significant overheating risk. The short circuit means a car running 5th-7th is almost always in another car's wake — rear wing temperatures and radiator inlet temperatures build rapidly. Agent should monitor overheating probability for lower-position drivers especially in the first 20 laps. ERS: High traction demand at altitude reduces ERS harvest efficiency — battery management more important than at sea-level tracks. Brakes: Downhill T1 braking is severe — high brake temps. T4 hairpin also generates high brake wear. Sprint format PU modes: if Sprint is included, teams run higher PU power modes in Sprint that can affect race reliability. Tyres: Graining possible on Mediums in cool morning conditions, but race temps typically warmer.

STRATEGY PATTERNS
Pit lane time loss: ~21 seconds. Short lap makes the undercut very powerful — losing position on track only costs 4.3 km, so pitting 1 lap early and gaining clean air is frequently the right call. 2-stop race: first stop typically laps 22-28, second stop laps 45-52. SC probability is so high that teams should always have a SC scenario plan. Cars on alternate strategies (long 1-stop) can benefit from SC but at the cost of pace risk on old tyres. Traction zones matter for soft compound performance — teams prioritise traction setup over aerodynamics.

WEATHER
Austria mountain weather: highly volatile. Rain probability: ~45% — afternoon thunderstorms common in June/July, particularly after 15:00 local. Rain arrives rapidly from the hills — radar is the most reliable predictor. Track temperature: 30-45°C in dry conditions. If rain arrives, intermediate compound comes in within 2-3 laps. Sprint format weekends have more unpredictability — a wet sprint changes tyre allocation significantly.

KEY CORNERS FOR AGENT ALERTS
T1 (downhill braking): Highest SC trigger corner. Heavy brake use on first lap and after restarts. Cold brake risk after SC periods.
T3 (Remus, high-speed right): Rear instability risk. Any rear deg shows up as understeer/snap here.
T4 (hairpin): Lockup probability high — uphill braking, easy to misjudge. Primary overtaking zone but also contact zone.
T6 (Rindt corner, medium-speed right): Rear traction loading — primary rear deg accumulation zone.
T9 (final hairpin): DRS activation, rear traction loading, contact risk on overtake attempts.
