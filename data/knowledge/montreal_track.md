# Circuit Gilles Villeneuve (Montreal) — Track Characteristics
The Circuit Gilles Villeneuve occupies Île Notre-Dame in the St. Lawrence River. It is a stop-start layout with two very long straights separated by heavy braking zones, making it one of the hardest circuits on the calendar for brakes. The infamous Wall of Champions (Turn 13 chicane exit wall) has ended the races of countless drivers and world champions attempting to extend their braking into the final chicane.

Key sectors:
- Sector 1: short technical section with tight hairpins; Turns 1–3 are slow and technically demanding; front-limited
- Sector 2: Casino Hairpin (Turn 10) and back straight; longest straight on the circuit (~1.1 km); major DRS and ERS zone
- Sector 3: Turns 11–13 hairpin–chicane–chicane complex; heaviest braking; Wall of Champions at Turn 13

Tire behaviour:
- Brake and traction dominated circuit. Rear tires are stressed by aggressive stops and exits from slow corners.
- Very low downforce setup is standard; this reduces mechanical grip through slow corners and amplifies rear tire wear.
- Soft compound can survive a 15–20 lap stint in cool conditions; medium is the reference race tire.
- Tire thermal stress is lower than high-speed circuits; the main limiting factor is mechanical wear from heavy braking and traction.

Safety car probability: Among the highest on the calendar. The Wall of Champions and pit lane entry complex generate regular safety car deployments; strategic flexibility is essential. Race pace is often set by safety car timing rather than tire windows.
