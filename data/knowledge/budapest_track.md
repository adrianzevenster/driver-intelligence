# Hungaroring (Budapest) — Track Characteristics
The Hungaroring is the tightest and most sinuous circuit on the modern F1 calendar — often described as Monaco without the walls. Its low average speed (~200 km/h) and lack of meaningful straights makes overtaking extremely difficult. The layout rewards mechanical grip, late braking, and setup precision. Qualifying position is critically important.

Key sectors:
- Sector 1: Turns 1–4; medium-speed entry into downhill left at Turn 2; Turn 4 heavy braking into hairpin; front-limited
- Sector 2: Turns 5–11; the slowest and most technical section; Turn 6 is the longest-duration apex corner; heavy rear tire wear through sustained traction zones
- Sector 3: Turns 12–14; uphill sequence including the kink at Turn 12; emerges onto back straight DRS zone; Turn 14 final corner exit is the key traction event

Tire behaviour:
- Rear tire wear is the dominant characteristic. The slow-speed traction zones through sector 2 continuously load the rear axle and generate heat.
- Front wear is secondary but present through the downhill sweepers in sector 1.
- Two-stop races are more common here than at most circuits because rear tires degrade regardless of compound.
- Undercut is effective because the tight track limits on-track overtaking — a fresh tire advantage matters more here than circuit position.

Key corner for engineers:
- Turn 4 (hairpin): Primary overtaking point. Any sign of rear instability here on exits indicates degrading rear tires.
- Turn 6 (sustained left): Monitor tire temperature in prolonged apex; highest single-corner thermal load on the circuit.
- Turn 14 exit: Key traction reference; rear snap here is a strong brake temperature + wear indicator.
