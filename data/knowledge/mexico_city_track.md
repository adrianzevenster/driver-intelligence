# Autodromo Hermanos Rodriguez (Mexico City) — Track Characteristics
Mexico City is one of the most technically unusual circuits on the calendar due to its extreme altitude of 2,240 metres above sea level. At this altitude, air density is approximately 23% lower than at sea level, which dramatically reduces aerodynamic downforce and engine power output. Teams must compensate with much higher-downforce wing settings, which partially restores mechanical grip but increases drag. The MGU-H operates under different thermal constraints and engine performance is reduced.

Key sectors:
- Sector 1: Turns 1–3 Peraltada-style long right-hander entry complex; high-speed flow compromised by altitude-reduced downforce; aerodynamic balance critical
- Sector 2: Stadium section; slow-speed Turns 6–13; tight technical inner circuit; mechanical grip domain; rear tire traction loading
- Sector 3: Back straight to Turn 16 hairpin; long DRS zone; Turns 17–20 high-speed sweepers onto main straight

Tire behaviour:
- Altitude reduces aerodynamic loads, which changes the nature of tire degradation. Mechanical wear increases relative to thermal wear compared to sea-level circuits.
- Rear traction wear through the stadium section is the primary degradation mechanism.
- Soft compound works better here in terms of longevity than at hotter circuits, as track surface temperature is lower (~28°C) despite the high ambient temperature.
- One-stop medium → hard is standard.

Engine and braking: Brakes run cooler in thinner air; engineers must watch for brake temperature below optimal range in sector 3. Engine torque is reduced; higher deployment rates of ERS per lap are standard to compensate.
